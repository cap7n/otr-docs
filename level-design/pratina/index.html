    Pratina¶

    Addendum to World & Story, session of 2026-07-14 (second half of the drive). This page answers the question World & Story left implicit: what, concretely, is the Prospector prospecting for? Everything here supersedes the abstract "value = arrangement" framing where the two differ - Pratina is that idea made into a substance.

    What it is (decided)¶

    Pratina is the residue that life leaves in matter. Living things locally build order in a universe that otherwise decays into disorder - and matter that sits inside a biosphere for long enough gets steeped in that, like tea. Soil that held forests for a hundred million years, seabeds, stone that carried cities: all of it carries a faint, harvestable charge of accumulated, lived order.

    The name (decided): a blend of prana (Sanskrit - life-force, the breath that animates living things) and patina (the surface character matter earns through age and use - the green film on old bronze, the sheen on handled wood). Each root carries half the definition; the blend says the whole thing in three syllables. Pronunciation to be locked before any barks or radio lines record - leading candidate: prah-TEE-nah.

    Why planets, why the crust (decided)¶

    • Asteroids have zero. Dead rock that never touched life. Raw material is the cheapest thing in existence to something that can cross spacetime; Pratina cannot be mined, only grown.
    • Dead planets hold traces - degraded, fossil Pratina.
    • Only the crust gets touched by life, so only the crust is worth cutting. This closes the "why not the core" question for good: the core is worthless, the crust is the plantation.

    Matter that was made and used by living beings is steeped twice. Every object in a junkyard was designed, handled, wanted, and discarded - a junkyard is one of the densest Pratina deposits a world produces. The scrapyard is canonically a rich sample. Surveyor drones going green over a rusted fridge is correct behavior.

    Economy consequence: loot value = Pratina density. Selling loot is selling concentrated lived-in-ness; prices are the structure's own appraisals (Economy & Loot).

    The droids run on it (decided)¶

    Droid batteries hold Pratina. The car's systems burn it. The structure harvests it at planetary scale. Three sizes of the same appetite.

    • The droids already know what the substance is - no discovery scene needed; they've run on it their whole existence. What they discover inside the structure is the scale of the appetite they share with it.
    • Gray-to-color is literal: a starving droid doesn't just lose power, it loses the ability to perceive the world's lived-in quality. Color drains because that is what Pratina is. (The existing gray-screen system and the intro's bloom-to-color now have a fictional root.)
    • The garage charger is a small refinery.
    • Closes the fuel open question: fuel is refined Pratina. Buy it through the salvager's resupply channel, or scavenge dense objects mid-run (The Car).

    The ripening model (decided - this is the load-bearing rule)¶

    Pratina works like the oxygen cycle: life produces it, and life consumes it. Two consequences:

    1. It must age. Fresh Pratina isn't usable; it has to mature in the matter that holds it.
    2. It spoils. Past the window, it degrades. There is a harvest moment, and missing it wastes the sample.

    This is why the zones shuffle. The structure isn't a conveyor (samples in, waste out) - it's a cellar. Zone shuffling is stock rotation: samples moved between long storage and short storage, checked, resampled, moved again to hit the window. Wine racks, cheese caves, crop rotation, at planetary scale.

    • The zones rearranging between visits is visible warehouse logistics, not arbitrary weirdness. Attentive players can learn to read it.
    • The diegetic exit lights are the rotation schedule in progress - which gates are open per cycle is which corridors are in use for rotation. (The exit-signal system now has a reason to exist beyond gameplay.)

    The livestock tension (decided in direction)¶

    Because life consumes Pratina too, a thriving biosphere is eating its own crop. The structure can't let sampled life run wild (it burns the harvest) and can't kill everything (production stops). It manages life like livestock - cull here, sustain there, hold each sample at the point where production beats consumption.

    • Enemies get a job description: not guards, not monsters - crop management. Every hostile machine is doing agriculture.
    • The droids are pests. In the structure's ledger they are shrinkage: two rats living in the cellar, drinking the wine - who then hacked the cash register. The whole game's fiction in one sentence, and it makes the salvager-audit response (see World & Story, the hacked salvager) feel inevitable rather than scripted.

    The structure, restated (decided)¶

    The Prospector is a prospector, refinery, and aging cellar in one. Its restlessness - the sampling, the shuffling, the crushing of spent zones - is its metabolism. Nothing it does is mysterious once you know what it's farming; it only looks incomprehensible because nobody inside has the full picture (see World & Story: Inhabited, but unknowable).

    Open threads (new since World & Story)¶

    • What does an overripe zone look like? A sample that missed its window and spoiled. If ripeness states map to zone aesthetics - fresh and vivid, peak and harvested, spoiled and dangerous - that's a visual language for every zone in the game, and the palette question from Open Questions ("how vivid, does it vary per zone?") answers itself: how vivid a zone looks is its Pratina state.
    • Pronunciation of Pratina - lock before recording anything.
    • Pratina in the structure's script: the alien language needs a glyph/symbol for it; it's the most common word on every salvager screen.
    • Whether players ever see a harvest - a zone at peak being drained is the biggest spectacle the fiction offers; even one scripted glimpse would teach the whole world model wordlessly.
